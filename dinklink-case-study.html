UI/UX Case Study

DinkLink: Pickleball Matchmaking

Building a mobile product that makes it easy for local players to find partners, organize matches, and keep up with their progress without getting lost in menus.

  • Role: Product Designer & Prototyper
  • Timeline: 6 weeks
  • Tools: Figma, FigJam
View full project Back to portfolio

Overview

DinkLink connects recreational and competitive pickleball players through a simple profile, matchmaking, and messaging experience. The goal was to reduce friction for pick-up matches while highlighting the stats and context players need to feel confident when reaching out.

Problem

Local players struggled to discover partners who match their skill, schedule, and preferred courts.

Outcome

A streamlined flow that surfaces nearby players, courts, and recent results in one glanceable feed.

Impact

Clearer onboarding, stronger profile signals, and faster conversations increased successful match requests in testing.

Discovery & Research

I interviewed five recreational players and mapped their current tools: text chains, Facebook groups, and ad-hoc spreadsheets to track scores. The common friction points were confidence in a partner’s skill, knowing which courts were free, and coordinating logistics without endless back-and-forth.

  • Players needed a quick read on skill level, preferred times, and recent matches.
  • Court availability and distance were key deciding factors when accepting an invite.
  • Messaging needed to stay lightweight, with match details attached to avoid confusion.

Design Approach

I built the interface in Figma with a focus on clarity: minimal chrome, strong typography, and modular cards that keep stats, partners, and court info aligned. I kept the visual language calm and confident so newcomers feel welcome while advanced players still get the details they expect.

Profile clarity

Progression badges, win/loss summaries, and common availability quickly communicate fit.

Matchmaking

Filters for distance, skill tier, and preferred format reduce the time to find a partner.

Messaging

Contextual threads attach location, time, and gear notes so players stay on the same page.

Solution Highlights

The final flow centers around a home dashboard that balances discovery with action. Players can move from browsing to inviting in under three taps while keeping court logistics transparent.

Home feed

Nearby partners, suggested courts, and recent matches appear in a single vertically scrollable stream.

Match request

Quick-select time blocks and skill tiers auto-populate invites to cut down on coordination messages.

Stats & history

Lightweight charts surface momentum without overwhelming casual players.

Building around reusable cards meant the system stayed consistent across the dashboard, player profiles, and court pages, making handoff easy and keeping the learning curve low.

Outcomes & Next Steps

Testers were able to create a profile and send their first invite in under three minutes. For a next iteration, I plan to pilot a lightweight availability calendar and integrate local club schedules.
